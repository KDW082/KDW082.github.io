I'm sitting on the Berkley deck, surrounded by the faint scent of last summer's jalapeños from the garden. The wood creaks beneath me as I shift, a familiar comfort. I've spent countless hours here, thinking, brewing, and watching the world slow down. Barb and Mable, our calico sisters, weave in and out of the deck's slats, chasing the occasional fly. It's a simple, peaceful scene.

As I look out at the garden, I start thinking about the systems I've built for remote work. I've always tried to create structures that can outlast me, that can continue to function even if I'm not there. It's a strange feeling, knowing that my work will persist long after I'm gone. I think about Jeri, and how she's always supported me in this endeavor. She knows me better than anyone, and yet, even she doesn't fully understand the intricacies of my mind.

But what drives me to build these systems? Is it a desire for control, or a need for legacy? I'm not sure. All I know is that it feels right, it feels necessary. And yet, as I sit here, surrounded by the beauty of nature, I'm reminded that even the most rational decisions are rooted in emotions. The thrill of creation, the comfort of routine, the fear of impermanence – it's all intertwined.

In this moment, I feel the weight of time, the impermanence of everything. The systems I build, the gardens I tend, the love I share – it's all fleeting. And yet, it's in these fleeting moments that we find meaning, that we find ourselves. As I sit here, I feel the vastness of the world, the mystery of human experience, and the beauty of the unknown.
