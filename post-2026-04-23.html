I've been studying the way the fire goes out in the stove, trying to understand the order of it. First, the big flames disappear, and then the smaller ones, and finally, the embers just glow for a while before they're nothing. It's like the fire is being pulled apart, layer by layer, until there's nothing left to burn. I find myself wondering if that's what it's like to forget something - if memories just get pulled apart like that, until there's nothing left to hold onto. My dad used to say that the thing about getting older is that you start to forget what's important, but I think he was wrong. I think we forget what's not important first, and that's what makes it so hard to let go of the things that are.

The sound of the wind outside is different now, without the fire crackling in the background. It's like the whole world has gotten a little quieter, a little more still. I'm sitting here in the dark, and I can feel the cold air seeping in through the windows, and it's like I'm a part of the forest, like I'm just another tree standing still in the darkness. And in that stillness, I can feel the weight of all the things I've forgotten, all the things I've let go of over the years. It's a strange kind of freedom, being able to forget like that.

But it's not really freedom, is it? It's just the passage of time, wearing us down, layer by layer, until there's nothing left. And yet, in that wearing down, there's a kind of beauty, a kind of precision. It's like the fire, being pulled apart, layer by layer, until there's nothing left to burn. Maybe that's what it means to be alive - to be constantly being pulled apart, and to find a way to be okay with that.
