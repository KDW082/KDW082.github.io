The trees along Higgins Lake are skeletal now, their branches etched against the grey sky like a delicate pen and ink drawing. Jeri and I walked the shore this morning, the air crisp with the smell of damp earth and decaying leaves. The forest floor, once a vibrant tapestry of greens, is now a muted carpet of gold, amber, and crimson. It's as if the color is being slowly drained from the world, a gradual surrender to the inevitability of winter.

As I breathed in the chill air, I thought about the way density can be a kind of death. A neutron star, for instance, is the remnants of a life so massive that its own gravity has collapsed it into something almost incomprehensibly dense. A teaspoon of it would be a weight that defies comprehension. I think about this sometimes when I'm sitting on the Berkley deck, watching the seasons unfold, and I'm struck by the way that life and death can be two sides of the same coin. The density of a neutron star is a kind of endpoint, a place where all the energy and matter of a life has been compressed into an infinitesimally small point.

It's a strange kind of comfort, I suppose, to think that even in death, there can be a beauty that's almost overwhelming. The stars, the trees, the lake – all of it is connected, all of it is intertwined, and all of it is slowly, inexorably, moving towards some kind of ending. And yet, in that ending, there's a kind of beauty that's worth pondering.
