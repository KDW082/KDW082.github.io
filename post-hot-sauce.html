Hot sauce and the guerrilla garden

On handing things over fences and what the backyard actually is.

← The Immortality Project
"Nature is what we know
Yet have no art to say
So impotent Our Wisdom is
To her Simplicity." — Emily Dickinson

The hot sauce started as a garden problem. You grow jalapeños and cayenne and holy basil and garlic chives and eventually the harvest outpaces the kitchen and you have to do something with it. The something turned out to be bottles. Small ones, labeled by hand, handed to people I know with no agenda except: here, I made this from the ground behind my house, I want you to have it.

That's the whole business plan. It's not a business. It's a philosophy with a stopper in it.

Dickinson was right — nature resists the art to say it. So I stopped trying to say it and started bottling it instead.

The chalet near Higgins Lake — Winter-Chard Lake Forest Chalet — is the same impulse at a larger scale. Jeri Chard and I built something worth sharing and so we share it. People rent it. They wake up to the lake. They stand at the water and feel what I feel when I stand at the water, which is approximately what Mary Oliver spent her whole career trying to articulate. The hot sauce and the chalet are the same gesture: here is a piece of the world I've been tending, come spend some time in it.

The guerrilla part is that neither of these things is primarily about money. The hot sauce is about the garden being too generous to keep to myself. The chalet is about a place being too good not to share. The marketing is just the mechanism — a way of saying to everyone I know: the backyard is real, the lake is real, you should see it.

I think about Thoreau at Walden — two years in the woods, then he came back and wrote about it so other people could go. The going matters. But the coming back and telling people matters too. You don't get to keep the good places to yourself. That's not what they're for.

The hot sauce is a small map. The chalet is a larger one. Both of them say: something worth finding is in this direction.

If you've never been to Higgins Lake in northern Michigan — the water is so clear you can see the bottom at twenty feet. The chalet sits in the pines. In the morning the light comes through the trees at an angle that makes everything look like it was arranged on purpose. It wasn't. That's the point. Nature has no art to say it. You just have to go.

winterchard.com — if you want to stay. The hot sauce, you'll have to know me for.
