The forest from
the mountaintop

On climbing, surviving, and the vanity that makes us useful to each other.

← The Immortality Project

There are two ways to know a forest. You can sit on a rock inside it — close to the moss, close to the dirt, one tree filling your whole view. Or you can climb until the canopy drops away and you can see the whole thing at once — where it thins, where it densens, where the river cuts through it, where it ends.

My mind has always wanted the mountaintop. The full picture. The system underneath the surface. I don't experience my thinking as chaotic — internally it runs clean, it runs straight, it sees the shape of things before the shape exists. What's messy is everything after that. The execution. The getting it out of my head and into the world in a form other people can use. That part I've had to redo. More than once. More than I wanted to.

The vision was always clear. The path up the mountain never was.

I chose to go to Mt. Pleasant over staying in the woods. That sounds like a metaphor. It was also literally true — there was a version of my life that didn't involve a degree, that stayed close to the rock, intimate with one small patch of ground. I didn't take it. Not because I was afraid of the rock. Because I understood, at some level I couldn't have articulated then, that a mind like mine needs infrastructure. Struggling with your mind inside an institution is survivable. Struggling for survival without one — that's a different kind of hard. That's the wild. I knew which fight I could win.

I got to Mt. Pleasant because I'd found logical contradictions in the Bible and nobody around me seemed bothered by them. I was bothered. That itch led me to a philosophy department, which I entered looking for answers and left understanding that the questions were the point. I wasn't drilling down on a career. I was drilling down on what was actually true. The institution was trying to hand me a vocation. I was trying to hand myself a foundation.

I wasn't the student who learned easily. I was the student who couldn't stop thinking — which is a different thing entirely, and the institution doesn't always know what to do with it.

Thoreau had a train running past Walden Pond the whole time he was there. He didn't pretend it wasn't there. He wrote about it — the intrusion of the industrial, the modern world refusing to respect the pastoral. That image has stayed with me longer than almost anything else I've read. Because the train is always there. The woods and the train. The mountaintop view and the messy climb to get there. You don't get one without the other. The question is just which one you're willing to let define you.

When I finally make it — when the execution catches up to the thought, when the thing I saw clearly at the start is finally out in the world and right — the feeling isn't pride exactly. It's closer to: made it. Now I know I can survive this mountain. And then, almost immediately, the next one appears on the horizon.

That cycle — the climb, the summit, the next peak — has vanity in it. I won't pretend otherwise. There's ego in wanting the mountaintop view, in needing to prove the mind can drag the body up there. But I've come to think that vanity, uncomfortable as it is to admit, is part of what makes people useful to each other. The uncomfortable experiences — the struggle, the exposure, the arriving somewhere hard — those produce something. A kind of heroism, small scale, daily. And a little of that is necessary. Not for the bottom line. For the thing bigger than the bottom line.

The vanity that drives you up the mountain is only worth something if what you do at the top is look around and figure out how to help someone else find the path.

There are more mountains. There always will be. That used to feel like a burden. Now it feels like the point.
