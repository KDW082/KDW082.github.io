The small metal clips that hold the electrical wires to the telephone pole outside the cabin are loose, just barely. If you look closely, you can see the wires sagging ever so slightly, as if they're trying to break free. I find myself wondering what would happen if one of them did come loose, if the entire system would collapse or if it would just be a small blip in the larger network. It's a question that feels both insignificant and crucial at the same time.

I remember my father telling me once that the biggest mistakes are often the smallest ones, the ones that seem insignificant in the moment but end up having huge consequences. He was talking about his work as an engineer, but I've found that it's true in all areas of life. A loose wire, a misplaced word, a misstep in a conversation - any of these can have a ripple effect that's impossible to predict. And yet, we often don't notice them until it's too late, until the damage is done and we're left to pick up the pieces.

As I stand here looking at the telephone pole, I'm struck by the fact that the loose clip is not just a potential problem, but also a reminder of the fragility of the systems we rely on. We take for granted the fact that our lights will turn on, our phones will work, and our cars will start, but it's all held together by tiny, invisible threads - threads that can be severed at any moment. It's a sobering thought, and one that makes me appreciate the beauty of the imperfect, the slightly-loose, and the almost-falling-apart.
