I've been watching the way the snow settles on the deck railing, how it forms a crust along the top and then gives way to a fragile, lace-like pattern where the wooden slats meet. It's as if the snow is trying to fill in the gaps, to make the railing whole again, but it can't quite manage it. The result is this intricate, imperfect lattice work that seems to defy gravity, at least for a little while. I find myself wondering about the temperature at which this happens, the precise point at which the snowflakes' ability to bond with each other and with the wood gives way to the gentle warmth of the sun.

My father used to talk about the importance of understanding the thresholds, the points at which one state gives way to another - the boiling point of water, the freezing point of lakes. He said it was the key to predicting behavior, to understanding how systems work. I never really understood what he meant, not until now, standing here watching the snow on the railing. It's not just about the temperature, or the material properties of the wood and the snow. It's about the way the world is full of these hidden thresholds, these points at which one thing becomes another. And it's the job of the person who's paying attention to find them, to map them out, and to understand how they work.

The snow is starting to melt now, the crust on the railing giving way to droplets of water that cling to the wood before falling, one by one, to the deck below. It's a slow, incremental process, but it's relentless. The lattice work is disappearing, bit by bit, and soon the railing will be bare again. But I know that the threshold is still there, waiting to be found, waiting to be understood. And I know that I'll be back out here tomorrow, watching the snow, looking for the point at which one thing becomes another.
