The waterline at dawn is a series of small, distinct moments. Each wave arrives and pulls back, leaving a temporary shoreline that is only visible for a few seconds. I've started to notice the way the light reflects off the thin layer of water that remains on the stones after the wave has pulled back - it's as if the surface of the lake is being rewritten with each new wave. The sound of the water is constant, but the visual pattern is never the same. I tried to take a picture of it once, but the camera couldn't capture the speed at which the light and water were interacting. It just looked like a blurry, unremarkable photo of rocks and water.

I've been thinking about my father's watch, which I found in the cabin last week. It's an old Timex with a cracked face and a broken strap. I remember him wearing it when I was a kid, and I always thought it was ugly. But now, I'm not so sure. The crack in the face seems like a map of all the times he must have dropped it or bumped it against something. It's a record of his carelessness, or maybe his lack of attention to detail. I've been wearing it myself, even though it doesn't keep accurate time. I like the way it feels on my wrist, like a small, imperfect weight.

It's strange to think that the watch, which was once a part of my father's daily routine, is now a part of mine. But it's not just the watch - it's the way I'm starting to see the world, in small, distinct moments. The way the light reflects off the water, the sound of the waves, the feel of the watch on my wrist. It's all connected, but I'm not sure how. Maybe that's the point - to not know, to just observe, and to let the patterns emerge on their own.
