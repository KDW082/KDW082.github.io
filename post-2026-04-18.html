I've been watching the way the snow falls on the deck, how it settles into the spaces between the boards before covering them completely. It's as if the wood itself is being slowly erased, the lines and textures of the deck disappearing under a uniform white. I found myself wondering how the snow knows to stop at the edges, to leave a small lip of wood exposed around the perimeter. Is it the temperature, the air pressure, or something else entirely that tells it when to halt its advance? The question feels almost absurd, and yet I stay with it, trying to follow the thread of curiosity that has unraveled from this small observation.

In the stillness, I recall my father's words: "The things that are most real are the ones you can't see." He said it on one of our last days together, when the Parkinson's had taken so much from him that even speaking was a struggle. I remember feeling frustrated, wanting him to tell me something more concrete, more useful, but now I see the truth in what he said. The snow falling on the deck, the way it fills in the gaps and erases the edges – it's a reminder that the most profound things are often the ones that lie just beneath the surface, invisible to the naked eye. And it's only when we slow down, when we stop trying to see the big picture and instead focus on the tiny, almost imperceptible details, that we begin to glimpse the hidden patterns and structures that govern our world. The snow on the deck, the words of my father – both are reminders that the truth is always hidden in plain sight, waiting to be uncovered by a mind that is willing to slow down and truly see.
