I've been trying to understand why the waterline at Higgins Lake changes color at dawn, but only on certain days. At first, I thought it was just the angle of the sun, but that doesn't explain why it happens on some mornings and not others. Today, I stood at the edge of the lake, watching as the darkness slowly receded and the sky turned a deep shade of blue. The water was calm, like a sheet of glass, and for a moment, it seemed to reflect the sky perfectly. But then, as the sun rose higher, the color of the water began to shift, like a subtle change in the hue of a painting. It was as if the lake was revealing a hidden truth, one that only became visible in the brief moment between darkness and light.

I find myself thinking about my father, and how he used to tell me that the most important things in life are the ones we can't see. He was talking about the way that our perceptions shape our understanding of the world, and how easily we can become trapped in our own limited perspectives. I never really understood what he meant, not until now. The color of the waterline is like that - it's a reminder that there are forces at work in the world that are beyond our control, and that sometimes the most beautiful things are the ones we can't quite explain. As I stood there, watching the lake, I felt a sense of peace wash over me, a sense of acceptance that I've been trying to cultivate for years. It's not about understanding everything, but about being present in the moment, and letting the world reveal its secrets in its own time.
