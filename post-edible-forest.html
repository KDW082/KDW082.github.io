The edible forest

The retirement plan. What the rock in the woods actually looks like.

← The Immortality Project

When I was nineteen I chose Mt. Pleasant over the woods. That was the right call — the mind needs infrastructure before it can afford to go feral. But I always knew the woods were the destination. Not as escape. As culmination.

The plan, when the time comes, is an edible forest. A designed landscape that produces things — food, medicine, material — the way a forest naturally produces things, except the species are chosen and the yields are intentional. Permaculture. Layers. Canopy to ground cover. Apples and berries and honey and maple and cedar, all of it working together the way ecosystems work, which is to say: slowly, interdependently, without needing to be told what to do.

Apples → butter + mead

Honey → infused honey + tea

Berries → dried + jam

Maple → syrup

Cedar / Pine → candles

This is not a business plan. It's a vision of what the hands do when the career is done. The same impulse that makes the hot sauce, that tends the garden, that harvests the holy basil on the Fourth of July — scaled up, slowed down, rooted in the ground near Higgins Lake where the water is clear enough to see the bottom at twenty feet.

The edible forest is the answer to the question I've been asking since I was nineteen. What does the rock in the woods actually look like? It looks like this. Productive. Patient. Designed to outlast the designer.

Thoreau went to Walden to live deliberately. He left after two years. I understand why — the world pulls you back, and there's work to do inside it. But the edible forest isn't Walden. It's not retreat. It's the thing you build after you've done the work, after you've climbed the mountains, after you've figured out which fight you can win. It's what you tend when tending is enough.

Dickinson said nature has no art to say itself. I've spent a career trying to say things for other people. When I'm done with that, I want to stop saying and start growing. Let the apples speak. Let the mead ferment. Let the cedar candles be the only light needed in a room where nobody needs anything explained.

The edible forest is the longest project. It takes decades to mature. Which means you plant it before you need it. You plant it for the version of yourself that's still coming, and for whoever comes after.

You plant it knowing you might not see it at its fullest. You plant it anyway. That's the whole philosophy.
