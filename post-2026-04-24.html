The ice on Higgins Lake is starting to break up, and I've been watching the way the large plates shift and grind against each other. It's a slow, almost geological process, but if you stand there long enough, you can see the movement. The sound is like a low, constant growl, and it's hard to tell where it's coming from. I found myself trying to pinpoint the source, as if it mattered. But it doesn't. The sound is just a byproduct of the ice doing what it's supposed to do.

I was thinking about my dad, and how he used to tell me that the hardest part of getting old wasn't the physical stuff, but the way time seems to speed up. He said it was like the days got shorter, but not in a way that you could measure. I didn't understand what he meant then, but I think I'm starting to. The ice breaking up on the lake isn't just a sign of spring coming; it's also a reminder that everything is in a state of constant, slow-motion collapse. The trees, the rocks, the ice – they're all just trying to find a way to come apart, to release the energy they've been holding. And we're no different.

I was standing there, listening to the ice, and I realized that my dad wasn't talking about time speeding up – he was talking about our perception of it. The days don't actually get shorter; we just get better at ignoring the things that don't matter. And that's when it hits you: the things that don't matter are the ones that make up most of your life. The ice will keep breaking up, the lake will keep freezing, and we'll keep trying to make sense of it all. But in the end, it's not about making sense – it's just about being there, standing on the shore, listening to the sound of the ice as it slowly comes apart.
