On Wit — and the armor we build

Margaret Edson's play about a woman who spent her life trying to outthink death. And what she got wrong.

← The Immortality Project

Margaret Edson's play Wit is about a John Donne scholar named Vivian Bearing who is dying of cancer. She is brilliant. Formidable. She has spent her entire career mastering the language of death — analyzing metaphysical poetry, parsing the semicolons in holy sonnets, building an intellectual fortress around the one fact none of us can argue our way out of. And then she's in a hospital bed, and the fortress doesn't help her at all.

That's the engine of the play. Not the cancer. The irony. The woman who spent forty years studying death in literature has no idea how to be with death in her body. The intellect that made her exceptional made her alone. The armor that protected her kept everyone out — including the parts of her own life that might have mattered.

"Nothing but a breath — a comma — separates life from life everlasting." Vivian spent her career on that comma. She never learned to live in it.

I understand her completely. I found logical contradictions in the Bible at nineteen and spent the next several decades in my own head, asking questions, building systems, analyzing the shape of things. The mountaintop mind. The thinker who had to learn to survive. There is a version of my life where I become Vivian — where the questions consume the questioner, where the study of mortality crowds out the living of a life.

What pulled me back — or maybe what was always different — was the specificity of small things. The hot sauce fermenting on the counter. The drive north on 127 after midnight. The morning at Higgins Lake when the water was so still it felt like the whole world had agreed to pause. Those things don't require a theory. They just require showing up.

Vivian's failure wasn't intellectual. It was attentional. She was looking at death so hard she forgot to look at the lake.

At the end of the play, what finally reaches her isn't a Donne sonnet. It's a children's book — The Runaway Bunny — read to her by her mentor. Simple. Warm. Utterly without armor. The thing she spent her whole career building defenses against turns out to be the only thing that matters: being seen, simply, by someone who loves you.

I asked Jeri to share this lifetime with me at a lighthouse on a peninsula jutting into Grand Traverse Bay. I didn't have a speech prepared. I just knew. That knowing — uncomplicated, undefended, just true — is the thing Vivian never quite got to. Not because she wasn't capable of it. Because she kept choosing the comma over the breath.

Becker would say Vivian's entire career was an immortality project — a way of building something that outlasts the body, of making the self feel permanent through scholarship. He would say the same about this site. He'd be right on both counts. The difference, I think, is knowing what it is and building it anyway. Eyes open. No armor. Fully aware that the lake at dawn is more honest than any theory about the lake at dawn.

You can study mortality your whole life. Or you can stand at the water long enough to understand what Mary Oliver was talking about. The lucky ones do both.

Vivian was brilliant. I'm glad she existed. I'm glad Edson wrote her down. But I don't want to be her. I want to be the person who made the hot sauce, asked the question, stood at the lake — and then, when the time came, wasn't surprised by any of it.
