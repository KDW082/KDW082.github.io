The ice on Higgins Lake is like a sheet of paper that's been crumpled and then flattened out again - all the lines and cracks still visible, but frozen in place. I was walking out on it this morning, the sun just starting to come up over the trees, and I noticed how the cracks seemed to be absorbing the light, like they were darker versions of the lines on a well-used map. It made me think about the way we draw maps, trying to impose order on a place, and how the lines on a map are always a little too straight, a little too clean. The cracks in the ice, on the other hand, were chaotic, unpredictable - they had clearly been made by the ice shifting and reforming itself, over and over, without any regard for human notions of neatness or simplicity.

As I stood there, I remembered a conversation I had with my dad, years ago, about the way that engineers try to control the flow of water - building dams, and canals, and levees, all in an effort to impose their will on a force that's fundamentally unpredictable. He was talking about how, no matter how hard they try, the water always seems to find a way to escape, to overflow, to make its way around the obstacles that are put in its path. And I think that's true, not just of water, but of everything - that the more we try to control it, the more it seems to slip through our fingers. The cracks in the ice, and the lines on a map, are both trying to tell us something about the limits of our control, about the way that the world is always going to be a little more complicated, a little more unpredictable, than we want it to be.
