Sitting on the Berkley deck, surrounded by the quiet of morning, I watch Barb and Mable chase the occasional fly. The city's distant hum is a reminder that even in stillness, the world keeps moving. I've spent years working from home, and the freedom is a double-edged sword. Without the daily commute, the structure of an office, I've had to create my own rhythm. Some days, it's a gentle hum, like the bees in the garden; others, it's a cacophony of distractions.

As I sip my coffee, I notice the way the light filters through the leaves, casting dappled shadows on the wood. It's beautiful, but my mind begins to wander. I think about the countless hours I've spent staring at screens, the endless stream of information that threatens to consume me. It's as if my attention is a small boat, adrift on a vast lake, susceptible to every ripple and wave.

In this quiet, I realize that my perception of reality is shaped by what I choose to focus on. The world is vast, yet my understanding of it is narrow, filtered through the lens of my own experiences. It's a strange kind of solitude, being alone with my thoughts, yet connected to the world through the fragile thread of my attention. As the sun rises higher, I'm reminded that even in stillness, the world keeps moving, and I'm just a small part of it, trying to make sense of the beauty and the noise.
