The ice on Higgins Lake is a different color at dawn, a pale blue that seems to come from underneath. I've been watching it from the deck, trying to understand how it changes as the sun rises. At first, it's almost black, but as the light creeps over the trees, the blue begins to seep in, like a stain spreading through the ice. I've seen this before, but it still surprises me every time - the way the color seems to emanate from within, rather than being reflected from above.

It reminds me of a conversation I had with my dad, years ago, about the way we perceive time. He said that our experience of it is like trying to read a book in a language we don't fully understand - we think we're getting the gist of it, but we're really just skimming the surface. I didn't think much of it at the time, but now, watching the ice, I feel like I'm seeing what he meant. The color of the ice isn't just a reflection of the light, it's a product of the ice itself, its molecular structure, its history. And our experience of time is like that too - it's not just a linear progression, it's a complex web of moments, each one influencing the others in ways we can't fully comprehend. The ice, like time, is a system that's both beautiful and unforgiving, and the more I watch it, the more I realize how little I really understand it.
